Common questions.

Aren't sun-grown microgreens more nutritious than indoor-grown?+

Short answer: there is no scientifically defensible blanket claim that sun-grown microgreens are inherently more nutrient-dense or longer-lasting than indoor vertically farmed ones. The answer depends on the exact variety, harvest timing, post-harvest handling, cold chain, packaging, and delivery time.

Natural sunlight can stimulate certain phytochemicals — anthocyanins, carotenoids, phenolics. But a well-designed controlled-environment system can intentionally increase vitamin C, polyphenols, anthocyanins, and glucosinolates by using targeted "light-stress recipes," elevated CO₂, and precise nutrient dosing. The deciding question is the measured nutrient assay per gram of identical SKUs at the same harvest age — not where they were grown.

A sophisticated vertical farm can absolutely outperform sun-grown systems on nutrient concentration for certain crops.
What about shelf life — doesn't a national brand last longer?+

This is where the answer becomes much clearer. Shelf life is mostly a cold-chain and elapsed-time problem, not a sunlight problem.

A product shipped from California to a Chicago restaurant goes through: harvest → precooling → packaging → trucking/air freight → distributor handling → restaurant receiving. Even with excellent cold chain, 2–5+ days of elapsed time is common before it reaches the kitchen.

Our model: harvested locally, delivered same day or next day. That gives dramatically lower respiration loss, moisture loss, chlorophyll degradation, and vitamin C decay. A product arriving already three days old cannot beat a product harvested eight hours ago — for cut greens, freshness is king.

Why does urban / controlled-environment agriculture matter?+

Three reasons working together:

  • Resilience. Indoor systems are immune to droughts, frost, wildfires, and seasonal shortages. Same crop, same week, every week.
  • Resource efficiency. Hydroponic systems use ~97% less water than field-grown, zero pesticides, and far less land per kilogram of output.
  • Proximity. When the farm is inside the city — or inside the building — fresh food becomes a default rather than a logistics challenge. Food is medicine when it's actually reachable.
How is FARMZER0 different from other indoor farms?+

Most indoor farms sit in warehouses on the edge of town and still truck their produce in. We do the opposite: we build inside vacant downtown office floors, where the food gets eaten. The result is the shortest possible supply chain — 14 floors, not 1,400 miles.

We also publish a transparent weekly Crop Report so chefs, hospitals, and home cooks know exactly what's on the walls, what's coming next, and can pre-order or request varieties.

Are microgreens really 4–40× more nutritious than mature plants?+

Yes — for specific nutrients in specific varieties, this range is well documented (Johns Hopkins / USDA-cited literature, 2023). Microgreens are harvested at the cotyledon stage when the plant has packed extreme concentrations of vitamins, antioxidants, and phytonutrients into its very first leaves.

Broccoli microgreens, for example, contain ~50–100× the sulforaphane concentration of mature broccoli florets. That's why we publish a Nutrition page where you can browse products by exactly the nutrient you're looking for.

Why open inside Chicago office buildings, specifically?+

Two reasons. First, Chicago has the highest downtown office vacancy on record — entire floors of beautiful buildings sit empty. Turning them into farms re-uses existing infrastructure (HVAC, plumbing, freight elevators) instead of building from scratch.

Second, in November 2024 Chicago became one of the first major US cities to permit indoor urban farming in all downtown DX and DC zoning districts. The regulatory door is open here in a way it isn't elsewhere yet.

How do you ensure food safety indoors?+

Cleaner inputs, fewer variables. No soil-borne pathogens, no animal contact, no irrigation water from open sources. Our seed lab pre-sterilizes every lot before sowing. Crews wear food-grade gloves and hair covers in grow halls. Every batch is sampled and the cold chain is unbroken from harvest to pickup.

We follow USDA Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) and HACCP protocols, even though we're not legally required to as a small grower — because it's the right baseline for produce eaten raw.

Is the food organic?+

Functionally, yes — we use organic seed, no pesticides, no herbicides, no synthetic fungicides. We use mineral-based hydroponic nutrient solutions. We're not certified USDA Organic only because that certification was written for soil-based farming and doesn't cleanly cover hydroponic CEA. We publish our full input list on request.
